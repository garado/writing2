— ONE —
Freewriting
Peter Elbow
This text proposes that freewriting is the most effective way to improve one’s writing because it allows for the unhindered expression of one’s natural voice. One of writing’s main advantages is that it allows more room for editing and revision, but if overdone and combined with a sense of perfectionism, it could be a major impediment and source of writer’s block. Elbow says that “in [one’s] natural way of producing words there is a sound, a texture, a rhythm - a voice - which is the main source of power in [one’s] writing,” and the process of editing places distance between the writer and that voice. Freewriting helps bring that voice to the surface instead of burying it beneath a need for perfection.
Elbow’s article especially resonates with me because the only way I can get things going when I have a particularly bad case of writer’s block is by freewriting. I feel like he was @ing me when he said that “trying to get the beginning just right is a formula for failure.” I am already actively applying the author’s suggestions to my own life and my own writing and will continue to do so in the future. I can also apply the key point of this text to other aspects of my life outside of writing - I don’t need to be perfect at everything; I just need to start.
— TWO —
They Say, I Say
Graff & Birkenstein
In this article, Graff and Birkenstein emphasize the importance of clearly stating one's point in addition to the reason why the point needs to be made. A good way to do this is to give the argument context: you should introduce what others are saying and only afterwards introduce your own ideas as a response. You can also summarize your ideas first briefly and then delay explanation for later.
— THREE —
Writing Speaks to Situations Through Recognizable Forms
Charles Bazerman
In this article, Bazerman states that how we communicate is dependent on the situation we're in and what kind of message we want to communicate. This process is harder to do in writing - there are "fewer here-and-now clues about audience [and] situation... so we need to understand the rhetorical situation." Understanding the rhetorical situation involves knowing genre, associated situations, and activity systems.
— FOUR —
Texts Get Their Meanings From Other Texts
Roozen
In this article, Roozen states that our interpretation of a text is heavily influenced by our prior working knowledge of other material (i.e. other texts, talks, visuals, etc.). He states that "forging and reconfiguring linkages to other texts and even other contexts can shift meaning in ways both subtle and profound."
