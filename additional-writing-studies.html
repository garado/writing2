Apostrophe Catastrophe (Part Two)
Grammar Girl
This article deals with how to make words that end in S possessive, as in Kansas’s vs. Kansas’. According to Grammar Girl, either way is perfectly acceptable and which to choose is simply a matter of style. AP style does not add an extra S after the apostrophe, whereas the Chicago style does (with a few very rare exceptions). I wholeheartedly appreciate this article because I have, despite major opposition from everyone and their moms and lots of red-pen corrections from past English teachers, long stood by my choice to add an extra S after the apostrophe. This is a hill I will die on. Thank you, Grammar Girl, for reconfirming the validity I always knew my stylistic choices had.

Ghost Words
Grammar Girl
In 1886, lexicographer Walter Skeat defined ghost words as words that had "no real existence." In other words, they're words that were accidentally made up through misreadings, transcription errors, or typos. One example is the word "syllabus," which comes from ancient Greece and was originally spelled "sittybas" or "sittubas." Somewhere along the way, someone miscopied it as "syllabus" and that's where we get the word today. I think the entire idea of ghost words being accidentally made up is so funny because all words (and all languages, for that matter) are made up anyways. I also think my boy Walter's definition of a ghost word is funny too because he's saying the words aren't real, and yet... there they are, in all their definitely existent glory, existing in whatever language the word exists in just to spite him.

Why Does ‘Love’ Mean ‘Zero’ in Tennis?
Grammar Girl
I've always wanted an answer to this question! I played tennis throughout high school and always wondered why it was called that. I certainly didn't love anything about having scored zero points. (Which was unfortunately very often - I wasn't good at tennis.) It turns out that nobody's 100% sure where the term comes from. One prominent theory is that the French word for egg is l'oeuf, and an egg looks like a zero, so someone decided to connect the two, and then another English-speaking someone decided that l'oeuf sounded like love, and the rest is history.

The History and Glory of the Spelling Bee
Grammar Girl
Let me preface this paragraph by saying that the fact that the English language even has a spelling bee is insane. I was in the Scripps National Spelling Bee when I was in middle school and I distinctly remember getting the ten-page packet of words to study and thinking, "Fuuuuuck that." There were random, extremely long, consonant-heavy German words in there and random other words from other languages that I didn't even know were languages and random words in English that definitely looked more like erratic keysmashes than words. It was when I was staring down the word "zeitgeist" for the hundredth time that my twelve-year-old self realized that English is for psychopaths. We have a language that's essentially the bastard child of five million other languages with words so horrendously difficult to spell that we pit hundreds of elementary and middle school children against each other in an annual fight to the death for linguistic clout. Crazy.
Complete bewilderment at the English language aside, I'm not sure how to feel about spelling bees. The article poses some good points - in the process of studying, participants learn about etymology, the history of the English language as a whole, and the value of hard work and determination. However, the whole ordeal definitely sounds like a playground for a batshit insane helicopter tiger mom to force their poor child into. It sounds like just another thing to put on a college application (and I hate when people do things just to put it on a college application). Spelling bees seem like something that people do for clout rather than for the love of the English language, and I definitely did it for clout back then. Can we all thank God that I've grown out of my clout-chasing phase? (And can I get $10 for every time I've used the word "clout" in this paragraph?)
The word that got me out, by the way, was adolescents - I spelled adolescence instead.

I.e. Versus E.g.
Grammar Girl
The other week, my linear algebra professor asked the class if anybody knew what "i.e." meant. I wish I'd read this article back then so I could've flexed my Latin on 300 people.
I've always wondered what the difference between i.e. and E.g. were, and this article has so graciously enlightened me. I.e. is an abbreviation for the Latin phrase "id est" and roughly means "that is." E.g. is an abbreviation for "exempli gratia" and means "for example."
